My Search for Truth: An Overview of the Contents of This Book

I needed to go back to the roots of Islam, back to the teachings of Judeo-Christianity (or, the “Ibrahimian religions” as I refer to the teachings of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam throughout this book). I found this approach necessary for three reasons. First, Islam claims it fulfills the Judeo-Christian message. So, I needed to know what Islam was fulfilling. Second, identifying how Islam is rooted in the JudeoChristian teachings shows how all three religions are closely connected. And third, the history of all three religions are full of examples of morally questionable actions, such as killing, massacring, plundering, enslavement, and the violation of human dignity and innate rights— these despite breaking the Ten Commandments, given by God to Moses in Exodus 20:13–17, most particularly “Thou shalt not kill.” These actions are not just specific to Islam. Moreover, if atrocious actions are part of all Ibrahimian religious histories, then a moral thinker ought to question the authenticity of the entire message of all these religions. If God is all pure, all good, and all perfect, then the God described in these religions ought to be as well. If the Ten Commandments truly show what God determines to be good (as the Ibrahimian religions maintain), then the merit of the Ten Commandments should resonate throughout their scriptures and histories, and each religion should be nothing except the goodness it proclaims.

To uncover God’s authentic message, I felt it necessary to examine the historical concept of God, and to provide an overview of the gradual development from polytheism (the concept of having many gods, which took on many shapes and forms) to monotheism (the concept of having a single, universal God). The first three chapters of this book are devoted to important early concepts God as conceived by three influential individuals: the great Ikhnaton, Zoroaster (or Zardesht, as his name is pronounced by those in his native area, and as he will be called throughout this book), and Moses. In the course of all my research for this book, I have come to the conclusion that a thorough examination of the ideas of Ikhnaton and Zardesht is crucial because the schools of thought that sprouted from these two individuals are responsible for most of the Ibrahimian literature.

The rest of this book explores the Ibrahimian scriptures in order to bring to the surface the morally questionable actions attributed to God, and examines the historical context surrounding the times when these actions were recorded to attempt to understand whether each religion’s message is as authentic as it claims. In other words, during the times of morally reprehensible actions detailed in Islamic, Judeo, and Christian scripture, was the scriptural message revealed by God, or rather did these revelations spring from the historical, political, or social impulses of the time?

(This book is my attempt to clarify these points. My hope is for an open-minded and thoughtful reader to start from a neutral position, see what is presented here, and assemble the conclusions for himself or herself. Consider God in the highest position, beyond and separate from the interpretation of any one of the Ibrahimian religions. Likewise, consider good and evil as moral questions higher than and separate from the interpretation of any one of the Ibrahimian religions.)
